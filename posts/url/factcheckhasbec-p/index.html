‘Fact check’ has become just another word for censorship

“Fact-checking” is like “fake news”: Something that is now entirely in the eye of the beholder.

Fact-Checking Becoming Censorship

Fact-checking has become just another word for censorship. In a recent vice presidential debate, CBS News moderators attempted to fact-check Sen. JD Vance and demonstrated one of the big problems in this media era: they only seemed interested in fact-checking in one direction - against the Republican candidate.

During the debate, moderator Margaret Brennan decided to fact-check something that Vance said and immediately relayed a piece of false information herself. This instance reveals that not only do these "fact-checkers" frequently get their facts wrong, but also they are selective about which facts to correct. In this case, it was clearly against Vance.

Issues with Media's Role in Fact-Checking

The problem lies in the fact that many media institutions are not competent at determining what is true or false. The era of social media has seen a dilution of power in traditional gatekeepers like newspapers and TV networks. New platforms allow anyone to point out inaccuracies, thereby questioning the previous control over narratives.

Media's Role: Gatekeeper vs. Story Selector

There used to be a time when newspapers could suppress certain stories, but with technological advancements, it is no longer possible to hide everything from the public eye. However, some media houses still try to select what to publish or not publish in order to suit a particular narrative, making them more untrustworthy than ever.

Public Awareness and Perception

The public has grown wiser in understanding these strategies. For instance, they can easily find a story that a media house tries to suppress by resorting to alternative platforms. But it also exposes the transparent and increasingly less trustworthy nature of such selective publishing houses.

When it comes to October surprises or other political tactics, people are not easily fooled anymore. They know that these tricks are expected during election times, so they do not fall for them as easily as before.

Conclusion

The fact-checking phenomenon has evolved into a tool for censorship in the wrong hands. Media organizations claiming neutrality and fairness often show clear biases or selectivity in their fact-checking approach, making it easier to identify their partisan leanings rather than being seen as unbiased or impartial gatekeepers of information.
